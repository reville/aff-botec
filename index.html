Addiction Frontier Fund BOTEC

A back-of-the-envelope cost-effectiveness estimate for the Addiction Frontier Fund: expected approvals, the lives and QALYs that follow, and cost per QALY.

Summary
  • A $250M fund should produce about one new or materially better addiction medicine that would not otherwise exist (range 0.3–3.0).
  • Base case: ~470,000 quality-adjusted life-years (QALYs) and ~28,000 lives saved, after crediting the fund a fair share of each win and counting a modest return of capital.
  • That is ~$530 per QALY and ~$8,800 per life, roughly 280× better than what US health payers treat as good value ($100–150k/QALY).
  • Benefit-to-cost ratio ~285:1 in the base case (range ~5:1 to ~10,000:1). Even the worst case beats that value bar by ~5×.
  • Every scenario is worth funding. The open question is how big the win is, and that turns mostly on per-medicine reach and the odds of approval.

The problem

Addiction is one of the largest under-treated health problems in the country, and it is starved of drug development.

The model

The chain
QALYs = shots-on-goal × P(approval) × QALYs/yr per medicine × years of benefit × credit share × capital recycled

Then: $/QALY = total fund cost ÷ QALYs, and benefit–cost ratio = (QALYs × value-per-QALY) ÷ cost.

  • We assume the fund mostly pulls a therapy forward in time rather than inventing one from nothing, and count the years of benefit gained, not the drug's whole future. [8]
  • The fund is one of several backers of any winning asset (co-investors, acquirers, NIH-funded science upstream). We credit it a share of the impact, 50% in the base case. That is higher than a pure funding-share rule, because in a field with almost no other early backers the fund is often the reason the asset exists at all. [8]

Costs

We use $250M as the base. The fund deploys over ~5 years; each medicine then pays out over a ~15-year window, discounted at 3%.

About one approved medicine from $250M is consistent with precedent. The two leading venture-philanthropy funds each produced roughly that: the Cystic Fibrosis Foundation got one transformative Vertex program from ~$150M, and the T1D Fund has one approval (Tzield) so far.

No scenario makes the fund a poor use of money. The worst case still buys health at ~$27,000/QALY, inside the range US payers fund every day, and returns ~5:1. The base case is far better, and the best case rivals the top global-health buys anywhere.

The full distribution

The Low and High columns above are scenario corners: every parameter pessimistic at once, or every one optimistic at once. Those are extreme tails, not a 90% interval. Treating each input as a lognormal centered on its Base value, with the tabled spread, and drawing 20,000 times gives the real distribution of cost per QALY.

Distribution of cost per QALY (20,000 Monte Carlo draws)
Log scale · dashed line marks the $150k/QALY US threshold

Across 20,000 draws: median …, 90% interval …, and a … chance of beating the $150k/QALY threshold. The spread is far narrower than the scenario corners because the parameters are roughly independent; for the result to approach the threshold, several would have to break the wrong way together.

Sensitivity analysis

We vary each parameter from Low to High, holding the rest at base, and recompute the cost per QALY. Longer bars are the parameters that matter most. The base case sits at the dotted line (~$530/QALY); left is cheaper, right is worse.

Tornado: cost per QALY ($), one parameter swung at a time
Lower is better · base case marked at $529/QALY

The dominant lever is per-medicine reach, how many people a new drug gets onto effective treatment, followed by the odds of approval and the credit share. More important for the conclusion: the worst end of the worst single parameter ($2,200/QALY) still beats the US threshold by ~70×, so the result is robust to any one assumption.

Why this is a lower bound

  • Co-investment. The T1D Fund turned $81M into more than $634M of private capital alongside it (~8×). Every AFF dollar pulls in others; the model credits the fund as if it acted alone.
  • Field effects. AI drug discovery, shared data, and a discovery engine make every future addiction program cheaper and likelier to work. That helps the whole field and is not priced here.

Key uncertainties

  • Attribution is the weakest assumption. Credit share and acceleration are judgment calls, not measurements. We set credit at 50%. A stricter rule pulls the base case toward the Low column, but not below the US threshold.
  • No addiction-specific success-rate data exists. We use psychiatry as the proxy for probability of approval (~14% from Phase 2), bounded below by neurology. Addiction could run worse; the repurposing-heavy portfolio (GLP-1s, orexin and kappa-opioid antagonists) argues it runs better.
  • Pricing health is not settled. Value per QALY ($100–150k), value per life-year (~$495k), and value per statistical life ($13.1M) give very different totals. We headline the most conservative, the $/QALY figure, and never mix them. [21]
  • One fund does not solve addiction. The base case averts ~28,000 deaths over ~15 years, on the order of ~1,900 a year at peak, against ~260,000 substance-related US deaths a year. The claim here is cost-effectiveness, not a cure. The math works precisely because the fund is small next to the problem and still moves it cheaply.
  • Per-medicine reach dominates the result. The whole estimate swings on the QALYs/yr term. If you think a new anti-craving or anti-overdose medicine reaches far fewer people than today's medicines do, lower that slider; the fund still clears the bar.
