Addiction Frontier Fund BOTEC

Summary — best guess
  • A rough expected-value BOTEC for a $250M fund. In the base case it backs ~9 serious shots, each ~15% likely to win approval: 1.35 approvals in expectation, a ~77% chance of at least one, and a ~23% chance of none.
  • Conditional on a win, a representative medicine might generate ~75k QALYs/year at US scale — the dominant and least-pinned-down assumption. After 12 discounted years of benefit, 50% counterfactual credit, and 1.5× recycled capital, that is ~911k expected QALYs, or ~$270/QALY charging the full $250M.
  • That clears US payer thresholds ($100–150k/QALY) by a wide margin — but that is a weak bar: per-medicine reach would have to be ~550× lower than base to fail it. The harder, more decision-relevant comparison is global-health philanthropy (GiveWell ~$80/DALY), where the US base case is roughly 3.4× more expensive per health unit and competes only via upside or global deployment.
  • The crux is not the valuation convention but whether AFF can cause at least one high-reach addiction medicine to exist roughly 5–10 years earlier than it otherwise would.

Six approvals in 23 years against 222 for cancer is the case for philanthropy, but burden alone does not make philanthropy the right marginal actor. Addiction is under-innovated for structural reasons: stigma and political economy, weak commercial incentives relative to the social burden, fragmented and under-resourced treatment delivery, payer and prescriber frictions, hard regulatory endpoints, and low venture appetite next to oncology and metabolic disease. Catalytic philanthropy is most useful where those frictions, rather than the underlying science, are the binding constraint.

The model

QALYs = shots-on-goal × P(approval) × QALYs/yr per medicine × years of benefit × credit share × capital recycled

Then: $/QALY = total fund cost ÷ QALYs, and benefit–cost ratio = (QALYs × value-per-QALY) ÷ cost.

  • Acceleration, not creation. The fund mostly pulls a therapy forward in time rather than inventing one that would never exist. The honest unit is credited years: if a medicine arrives in year X with the fund and year Y without, the contribution is (Y − X) years of benefit, weighted by the chance it would have happened anyway. We fold that into two parameters — the 12 discounted-equivalent years of benefit (a medicine otherwise arriving ~5–10 years later, or not at all in the most neglected sub-fields) and the 50% credit share. Pure acceleration of an asset pharma would have funded regardless belongs near the Low credit end. [8]
  • The fund is one of several backers of any winning asset (co-investors, acquirers, NIH-funded science upstream). We credit it a share of the impact, 50% in the base case. That is higher than a pure funding-share rule, because in a field with almost no other early backers the fund is often the reason the asset exists at all. [8]
  • Co-investment is internalized, not a bonus. The ~$20M cost per shot is the fund's catalytic slice; co-investors and acquirers fund the rest of each asset's roughly $1–2.6B path to market. That is also why the credit share is ~50%, not 100%. We do not separately count co-investment as upside.

We use $250M as the base. The fund deploys over ~5 years; each medicine then pays out over a ~15-year window, discounted at 3%.

[13] QALYs/yr decomposes into mortality (lives/yr × ~9 quality-adjusted life-years per death averted, reflecting the ~15-year OUD life-expectancy gap) plus morbidity (incremental treated patients × ~0.08 QALY/yr). About 1.35 approved medicines from $250M is consistent with precedent, and the estimate already prices in failure: about 85% of funded shots never reach approval, so the full $250M is charged against ~1.35 expected wins. The two leading venture-philanthropy funds each have one transformative approval so far (the Cystic Fibrosis Foundation's CFTR modulators; the T1D Fund's Tzield), both alongside other bets that have not yet produced a drug. CFF's often-quoted ~$150M was its own catalytic stake in the Vertex program, not the far larger total cost Vertex and its investors spent to finish the drugs.

Approvals are discrete — expected value is not a realized outcome

"1.35 approvals" is an expectation. A real portfolio of 9 shots at 15% each is a binomial draw, and the realized outcome is lumpy:

Portfolio outcome (9 shots × 15%)Value
Expected approvals (first cycle)1.35
P(0 approvals)~23%
P(exactly 1)~37%
P(≥1 approval)~77%
P(≥2 approvals)~40%

So roughly a quarter of the time the fund produces no approval at all and its realized cost-effectiveness is undefined; the expected-value figures already average that case in. Recycling is the same story over the fund's life: the 1.35 is first-cycle, returned exits fund roughly another 0.7 approvals' worth of shots, and the 1.5× recycling multiplier in the chain corresponds to ~2.03 total expected approvals credited to the fund (1.35 first-cycle + ~0.68 from recycled capital).

Where the QALYs/year come from

The 75k base is the dominant and least-grounded input, so it should not stay a single number. It decomposes as:

QALYs/yr = incremental patients on effective treatment × (baseline mortality × risk reduction × QALYs per death averted + morbidity QALYs per patient-year)

Worked example, an OUD adherence medicine: ~5M with opioid use disorder × ~5 points of incremental coverage ≈ 250k patients; × (3.6%/yr untreated mortality × 55% reduction [14] × ~9 QALYs per death averted + ~0.08 morbidity QALY/yr) ≈ ~65,000 QALYs/yr. The 75k base sits just above this; the 450k High is the polysubstance GLP-1 in the last row.

Addiction is not one homogeneous opportunity. Archetypes differ sharply in reach, effect, neglectedness, and counterfactual credit:

"Gross" QALYs/yr are before the 50% credit and the years/recycling multipliers. Tobacco is the clearest split: large gross health but low counterfactual credit, because effective drugs already exist — so a tobacco-weighted win should be credited near the low end, and the fund's real tobacco leverage is deployment or a genuinely novel (e.g. GLP-1) mechanism. The base case is a credit-weighted blend nearer the OUD/AUD archetypes than the GLP-1 upside.

Adoption is not automatic

A medicine produces QALYs only after a long cascade, and addiction is where this cascade leaks most:

approval → guideline inclusion → reimbursement → clinician adoption → patient initiation → persistence / adherence → clinical effect

The treatment gaps in the background table are this cascade failing for drugs that already work: only ~18% of people with OUD are on medication, under 2% for AUD, and fewer than one in three quit attempts use any cessation drug. A new molecule that is approved but lands in the same delivery system inherits the same leak. The "incremental uptake" column in the archetype table is doing all the work, and it is an assumption, not a measurement — so realistic per-medicine reach could be well below base, and the deployment/access channel (below) may matter as much as discovery.

If deployed globally

The model counts US health only. Drug development is a one-time cost, but an approved medicine can be used worldwide. The US has about 4% of the world's population and about 7% of its addiction deaths; the rest of the burden is abroad. Global addiction mortality is roughly 11 million deaths a year (tobacco ~8M, alcohol ~2.6M, drug use disorders ~0.6M; WHO and GBD), about 15 times the US total. [23]

The ~$250M of development does not change, so health and cost-effectiveness scale with the population reached. Matching the medicine's US fractional uptake worldwide would be about 15× the US health (~11 million QALYs, ~700,000 lives, ~$25 per QALY); reaching one-third of that uptake would be about 5× (~3.5 million QALYs, ~240,000 lives, ~$70 per QALY). Both fall within the range of the most cost-effective interventions in global health (about $50–100 per DALY). This is the develop-once, deploy-worldwide logic behind MMV's antimalarial record. [19]

This is an upper bound. Global reach is limited by access, affordability, regulatory approval in each country, and health-system capacity, and the fund's counterfactual credit for deployment by others is weaker than in the US. The US figures remain the headline.

Ways this could be wrong

An EA-adjacent reader should weight these at least as heavily as the headline. The model can be roughly right and the opportunity still disappoint, mostly through structure rather than the parameter ranges above.

  • Adoption, not approval, is the real bottleneck. If new medicines land in the same leaky delivery system (stigma, payer restrictions, prescriber capacity), realized reach is far below the uptake assumed in the archetype table, and per-medicine QALYs/yr could be a fraction of base.
  • Counterfactual credit is too high. If pharma, NIH, the GLP-1 sponsors now entering AUD (Lilly, Altimmune), or existing addiction companies would have advanced the same assets, the fund mostly buys a modest speed-up and credit belongs near the Low end or below.
  • Substitution, not expansion. An approved medicine may displace an existing effective one rather than add net treatment, in which case incremental QALYs are much smaller than the gross figures.
  • Approval base rates are the wrong proxy. Psychiatry/neurology success rates may overstate addiction assets, which face harder endpoints and weaker biomarkers.
  • Access and pricing erode reach. IP, list price, and coverage decisions (acute for GLP-1s) can keep population reach low even after approval.
  • The portfolio realizes zero approvals. About 23% of the time, despite positive expected value, the fund produces no drug and its realized cost-effectiveness is undefined. Expected-value framing is correct but hides this.
  • Downside and harm are possible. Addiction medicines carry tail risks — abuse liability, diversion, adverse events, substitution away from better-proven treatments, political backlash, or overpricing that suppresses access. None are likely to dominate, but a serious BOTEC names them.
  • It does not solve addiction. The base case averts ~4,050 US deaths a year at peak against ~740,000 — a cost-effectiveness claim, not a solution.
  • Valuation is a convention. Value per QALY ($100–150k), VSLY (~$495k), and VSL ($13.1M) differ by ~50×; we headline the lowest and never combine them. [21]

Outside view vs inside view

Outside view (base rates): addiction/psychiatry development succeeds ~12–15% from Phase 2; CNS Phase 2 trials cost ~$14–25M; new addiction medicines reach a small share of the eligible population. On these alone, AFF is an ordinary, risky biotech portfolio.

Inside view (the case for AFF specifically): it can select unusually neglected and repurposable assets, supply catalytic early capital where almost no one else does, and work the deployment side. The estimate leans on the inside view for credit (50% rather than a lower funding-share) and for above-floor approval odds. A reader skeptical of AFF's sourcing or edge should discount toward the outside view, which pushes credit and P(approval) down and the result toward the Low column.

Channels and what is counted

To keep the conservatism legible rather than rhetorical, here is what the headline does and does not include.

ChannelCostIn the headline?
Medicine development (the modeled chain)$180MYes — this is the BOTEC
Full-fund conservative denominator$250MYes — only medicine benefits counted, full fund charged
Deployment / access (close the treatment gap)$40MNo — plausibly the highest-value dollar, given the leaky adoption cascade; a separate BOTEC
Discovery infrastructure (AI targets, data)$20MNo — option value / field-building
Field spillovers—No — cheaper, likelier future addiction programs
Charging the full $250M against the medicine channel alone is the conservative choice — it makes the medicine-only $/QALY look about 1.4× worse than if charged on the $180M actually spent on shots — and we use it as the headline rather than as a separate boast. Deployment may be the highest-value channel given how leaky the adoption cascade is, but it is not in the headline figure.

Is gross capital the right denominator?

For a venture-style fund the social cost is arguably not the full $250M gross. Three denominators bracket it: gross deployed capital ($250M, the headline); expected net philanthropic cost after returns (lower — exits and recycling return capital, so the loss-adjusted subsidy is smaller); and donor opportunity cost (the next-best use of the same dollars). We use gross capital because it is the most conservative and least assumption-laden; a net-of-returns denominator would improve the apparent cost-effectiveness, and the recycling multiplier already captures part of it.

What I would research next

The highest-value diligence is on the inputs that are assumptions rather than data:

  • Addiction-specific clinical success rates by modality and phase, versus the psychiatry/neurology proxy.
  • The actual venture and philanthropic funding gap by asset class — is addiction as abandoned as the approval count implies?
  • Adoption elasticity: empirical uptake curves for varenicline, buprenorphine formulations, naltrexone, nalmefene, and off-label GLP-1 use — does a better molecule actually move coverage?
  • Payer and reimbursement barriers, and what it costs to move them (the deployment channel).
  • Expert elicitation on counterfactual timelines, with versus without AFF, for a handful of concrete assets.
  • Whether AFF has enough proprietary sourcing advantage to justify credit above a pure funding-share rule.
Bottom line

The case for AFF is not that the base case is precise — it is not — but that addiction medicine appears unusually important and neglected, and a venture-philanthropy portfolio has plausible hit-driven upside. The base case clears US payer thresholds by a wide margin, but that bar is nearly unconditional and carries little information. The decision-relevant question is whether the fund can cause at least one high-reach medicine to exist 5–10 years earlier than it otherwise would, and at what counterfactual credit. The most valuable diligence is on reach/adoption and counterfactual credit, not on refining the $150k/QALY valuation.
