Addiction Frontier Fund BOTEC

Summary, best guess
  • A rough expected-value BOTEC for a fixed $250M R&D and portfolio-development cost. In the base case it backs ~9 serious portfolio programs, each ~15% likely to win approval: 1.35 first-cycle approvals in expectation, a ~77% chance of at least one, and a ~23% chance of none.
  • In the default global partial-access case, the model produces ~5.7M expected QALYs, ~380k deaths averted, and ~$44/QALY using the full $250M denominator. The narrow US-only floor is ~1.14M QALYs, ~75.9k deaths averted, and ~$220/QALY.
  • The key question is whether AFF can cause a high-reach medicine to exist years earlier than it otherwise would, and deserve meaningful counterfactual credit for that acceleration.
  • The upside depends on reachable deployment rather than burden alone: rich-country diffusion implies ~$110/QALY and broad global access ~$15/QALY, conditional on affordability, regulatory approval, prescribing, adherence, and procurement.

The model

QALYs = portfolio programs × P(approval) × QALYs/yr per medicine × years of benefit × credit share × capital recycled

Then: $/QALY = total fund cost ÷ QALYs, and benefit–cost ratio = (QALYs × value-per-QALY) ÷ cost.

  • The fund mostly pulls a therapy forward in time, but some addiction-medicine candidates may not be investigated at all without a field-building funder. The honest unit is credited years: if a medicine arrives in year X with the fund and year Y without, the contribution is (Y − X) years of benefit, weighted by the chance it would have happened anyway. We fold that into two parameters: 15 discounted-equivalent years of benefit and the 50% credit share. Pure acceleration of an asset pharma would have funded regardless belongs near the Low credit end; an otherwise-unfunded candidate belongs nearer the base or high end. [8]
  • The fund is one of several backers of any winning asset (co-investors, acquirers, NIH-funded science upstream). We credit it a share of the impact, 50% in the base case. That is higher than a pure funding-share rule, because in a field with almost no other early backers the fund is often the reason the asset exists at all. [8]
  • The ~$20M cost per portfolio program is the fund's catalytic slice; co-investors and acquirers fund the rest of each asset's roughly $1–2.6B path to market. That is also why the credit share is ~50%, not 100%. We do not separately count co-investment as upside.

Cost, impact, and value under different deployment scopes

The $250M is treated as a one-time portfolio development cost, while the same approved medicine can in principle generate health benefits in the US and abroad. The same cost denominator is therefore used across deployment scenarios. What changes is how much population-level benefit the approved medicine eventually reaches.

The US-only case is the narrow, conservative deployment scope. The global cases are illustrative deployment scenarios, not forecasts. They are conditional on access, affordability, regulatory approval, adoption, and a counterfactual credit judgment about benefits realized outside the fund's direct control.

QALYs/year calculation

The 75k base is the dominant and least-grounded input, so it should not stay a single number. It decomposes as:

QALYs/yr = incremental patients on effective treatment × (baseline mortality × risk reduction × QALYs per death averted + morbidity QALYs per patient-year)

For example, an OUD adherence medicine: ~5M with opioid use disorder × ~5 points of incremental coverage ≈ 250k patients; × (3.6%/yr untreated mortality × 55% reduction [14] × ~9 QALYs per death averted + ~0.08 morbidity QALY/yr) ≈ ~65,000 QALYs/yr. The 75k base sits just above this; the 450k High is the polysubstance GLP-1 in the last row.

The Low and High columns above are scenario corners. The Monte Carlo instead samples each input as a lognormal centered on its Base value with the tabled spread, and samples the number of approvals as a discrete binomial draw (9 programs × 15%) rather than a smooth 1.35. This matters because zero-approval portfolios are not tail trivia; they are a central feature of the opportunity.

Ways this could be wrong

  • Counterfactual credit is too high. If pharma, NIH, the GLP-1 sponsors now entering AUD (Lilly, Altimmune), or existing addiction companies would have advanced the same assets, the fund mostly buys a modest speed-up and credit belongs near the Low end or below.
  • Substitution, not expansion. An approved medicine may displace an existing effective one rather than add net treatment, in which case incremental QALYs are much smaller than the gross figures.
  • Approval base rates are the wrong proxy. Psychiatry/neurology success rates may overstate addiction assets, which face harder endpoints and weaker biomarkers.
  • Access and pricing erode reach. IP, list price, and coverage decisions (acute for GLP-1s) can keep population reach low even after approval.
  • Global deployment does not materialize. The 2×, 5×, and 15× reachable multipliers are illustrative deployment sensitivities, not forecasts. Regulatory approval, affordability, procurement, supply, prescriber capacity, and adherence could keep realized global benefit much closer to the US-only case.
  • The portfolio realizes zero approvals. About 23% of the time, despite positive expected value, the fund produces no drug and its realized cost-effectiveness is undefined. Expected-value framing is correct but hides this.
  • Downside and harm are possible. Addiction medicines carry tail risks: abuse liability, diversion, adverse events, substitution away from better-proven treatments, political backlash, or overpricing that suppresses access.

Outside view vs inside view

Outside view (base rates): addiction/psychiatry development succeeds ~12–15% from Phase 2; CNS Phase 2 trials cost ~$14–25M; new addiction medicines have historically been low efficacy or high side effects and have reached only a small share of the eligible population. On these alone, AFF is an ordinary, risky biotech portfolio.

Inside view (the case for AFF specifically): it can select unusually neglected and repurposable assets, supply catalytic early capital where almost no one else does, and focus on assets that have higher appeal and adoption opportunities. The estimate leans on the inside view for credit (50% rather than a lower funding-share) and for above-floor approval odds.
